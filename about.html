Cube Judo

A teaching method for judo developed by Munkhtulga Battogtokh

Judo happens in the real world — it's complex, there are many variables to consider, and even when you know those, it's full of uncertainty. That makes teaching it very hard, and genuinely interesting. The challenge is to explain techniques as simply and as accurately as possible — and I've been developing my own way of thinking about judo from the first principles of physics and biomechanics to do that.

Rotation

The goal in judo is to throw your opponent onto their back. This means rotating them. No matter how they got there, if a person started standing and ultimately ended on their back, there was rotation involved in the middle. This is my first insight behind Cube Judo.

When you think simple like that, you realise all you want is for your opponent to be rotated 90 degrees backward. But you know it's not that trivial in reality. That's because first of all there are three axes of rotation.

In judo, techniques are rehearsed in forward or backward, sideways right or left, or in rotating motion clockwise or counter clockwise. Those correspond to the three rotation axes. Rotations at diagonal angles can be simply thought of as rotation across multiple axes rather than one (this one takes some prior physics knowledge to understand but no sweat if you don't understand what this means right now).

The bottomline is that three axes of rotation means there are many ways to get someone on their back. With uchimata (inner thigh reap), a very fast way to finish is to rotate your opponent's torso 90 degrees forward, and then 180 degrees horizontally once their back is facing upward at the ceiling. With ippon-seoinage (shoulder throw), you rotate them 270 degrees forward by throwing them straight forward over your own body, which is net 90 degrees backward. Breaking down the rotations in techniques this way allows you to understand what variations they may exist for a given technique and more importantly, to have a simple clear understanding of why a coach says what they say: pull with this arm, push with that arm, or reap with this leg, and so on. Beginners often follow micro instructions without understanding the purpose and do counterproductive movements. Clear understanding of rotations can prevent such waste and any resulting bad habits.

Simplification

I have already applied explaining the rotations involved in a technique in my own coaching experience. It resulted in the cleanest uchimata I have ever seen a beginner do, which encouraged me to develop it further.

However, although it was simple enough for people to understand when I explained the rotations involved in a single technique, it was not easy to explain the whole concept unless they already knew the physics of rotation. Furthermore, mental arithmetics with numbers like 90 and 270 is non-trivial, even for me (shocking).

The good thing is since we speak in 90 degree increments, we can just think of that as 1 flip so that the goal is to simply make opponent do net 1 flip. The moment I thought of this is when I realised if we're only talking about rotation, we can simplify the thinking by abstracting a person's body shape into a cube (I know, crazy).

Imagine a white cube representing a person with one side painted blue, the blue side representing the back. The blue side can either face up at the ceiling, or sideways at the walls if it's not already facing the ground. That's true about a person. Their back can only be either facing the ceiling, the walls, or in between if not the floor. Furthermore, no matter which way the cube is facing, you need only three flips in total to get the blue side facing down. So in practice, you don't need to wheel somebody right five times and then left six times. At any given point, you need only three flips at most (or 270 degrees) and you can easily decide which way (axis) is the best way to rotate them.

Reversing the simplification

Of course, the single cube is the simplest possible way of thinking and as we concern ourselves with more factors than just rotation (like stance width, height, etc.), we can undo the simplification one step at a time. For example, to consider height as a factor, we can use a 2-cube model rather than a single cube. This immediately gives more insight and reflects reality more accurately since not all axes of rotation are equal. Since a human body has a long shape, rotation across that length is the fastest (look into why figure skaters bring their arm in to spin faster, or the sport science literature, which I myself am quite new to, for deeper dive). After this 2-cube model, we can use a 3-cube model to take bending into account, and after that, we can incorporate the two legs separately into the model and so on to gradually increase our understanding.

Conclusion

Cube Judo teaches both judo mechanics and abstract thinking at the same time. For judo coaches, it can be an accessible teaching tool that even young children can understand. For problem solvers, it is an example of how to apply mental flexibility to boil a complex real world problem down to its essence, and how to re-introduce complexity afterward to address limitations of the simplified model. Although it came to me based on my knowledge of physics of rotation, for those new to the physics and the maths, it can be used as an accessible way to introduce the concepts through a concrete real world example.
